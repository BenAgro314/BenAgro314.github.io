Mind Worms

I am often afflicted by what I like to call Mind Worms 1: thoughts that I can’t seem to get out of my head, and grow to occupy the majority of my working memory. For example, my2 mind worms often take the form of:

  • a technical problem that I have yet overcome
  • a post-exam obsession/stress over a question I was unable to solve
  • a social or personal anxiety that has been recently aggravated

In my experience, mind worms can either

  • grow gradually through a persistent mental nagging that increasingly draws my attention away from what I want to be focusing on
  • or spontaneously proliferate via an external stimulus (e.g a social media post) or an internal realization (e.g “oh shit I sketched that graph wrong on my test!")

This is not to say that mind worms are inherently bad. Indeed, sometimes my most satisfying and brilliant epiphanies come from the solution to a mind worm problem. Moreover, the all consuming power of a mind worm can serve one well in a high pressure situation where the solution to that problem is all that matters.

However, I think that these benefits are relatively rare, situational, and are not exclusively brought about by mind worms. Epiphanies can come without the associated stress that mind worms cause, and focus under pressure can be achieved through mindfulness and flow 3. Throughout my (ongoing) struggle with mind worms, I have gained some experiential knowledge on their prevention, eradication, and aggravation/proliferation. In this post I will try and share what I have learnt.

Prevention

The birth and subsequent growth of a mind worm is highly dependent on your mood and emotions surrounding the instigating thought. Thus, if you can cultivate a healthy mental context around potentially worm-inducing thoughts and activities, the chance of developing a mind worm will be reduced. While there are many different methodologies for changing your state of mind, the one I will discuss is mindfulness.

Mindfulness

While I have practiced mindfulness for nearly 6 months, I am by no means the best person to explain it, so I will instead introduce this rich topic, explain its connection to mind worms, and direct you to external resources for further exploration.

Mindfulness is a “state of open, non-judgemental, and nondiscursive attention to the contents of conciousness” (Harris). Mindfulness asks you to turn your spotlight of conciousness inwards, acknowledging thoughts, emotions, and sensations as they arise and pass away, as if from a 3rd party perspective. While on the surface a simple concept, achieving a state of mindfulness for a prolonged period is paradoxically difficult. You will notice (or perhaps not notice), just how easy it is to become lost in your own thoughts for many minutes.

Mindfulness is generally practiced in a semi-formal meditation setting: sitting down in a comfortable space with your eyes closed. However, the power of mindfulness lies in its applicability in any setting. Invoking a brief state of mindfulness (which I often initiate with a deep breath) can drastically reduce the “half-life” of a thought with mind worm potential. When such a thought begins to arise, examine it from the third-party perspective of mindfulness for what it is: just an appearance in conciousness. Mindfulness allows you to intervene in the self-talk of the mind, stopping the growth of a mind worm in it’s tracks.

Mindfulness is useful for removing mind worms that have already taken root, although prevention is easier than eradication.

Here are some of the best resources I have come across for learning the theory and practice of mindfulness:

  • Waking Up: A Guide to Spirituality Without Religion by Sam Harris. This is a book about spirituality without religion, and describes much of the theory of mindfulness.
  • The Waking Up App. For those that are looking to move beyond hypotheticals, this app provides guided daily guided meditations of varying duration, along with audio lessons to bolster your understanding of the theory behind various meditation practices. While it is subscription based, it is a pay-as-you-can model.

Sleep

Mind worms feed on fatigue and the delirium associated with over-working. Good sleep hygiene is simple to understand, but the habits can be difficult to build. Make it a goal to develop a practical sleep routine, and follow it for at least a month to make sure you have the habits engrained. Some of the basic tips are:

  • avoid caffeine late in the day
  • avoid alcohol and nicotine
  • exercise regularly
  • reduce noise and light
  • reduce screen time before bed
  • have a regular sleep timing

The two points can be particularly difficult for busy people, but I cannot stress their importance enough. The time you gain from staying up late to work is almost always not worth the effect it will have on your energy levels 4. I choose to save on-paper work for the evening and ensure my screens shift to night-time mode a few hours before bed.

A good night’s sleep (as opposed to a daytime nap) is also an excellent way to hit the mental reset button and clear a mind worm.

Eradication

What are some other, perhaps less esoteric, techniques that you can implement right now to kill a pesky mind worm?

Exercise

One of the best ways to break out of the recursive thought-loop of a mind worm is to think about something else. This is far easier said than done, because the mind-worm will always try and drag you attention back. By forcing your mind to control your body through exercise, you can take your brain into manual mode. Most people will find exercise to be a more tractable solution than mindfulness, it is conceptually simple to understand what you have to do.

Sadly, exercise itself is not something that is easy or pleasant to start, and the most fun exercises (i.e. sports) usually require friends, equipment or a special setting. However, something as simple as a walk or some pushups is usually effective. As long as you can get your blood pumping, you will begin to flush that mind worm out!

Socialization

Talking with friends and family allows one to

  • gain a wider perspective on issues that internally seem all-encompassing
  • empathize with the experiences of others and inject some of their thoughts into your own mind

Socialization can help you kill a mind worm by outsourcing some remedial perspective from others. Regular social contact also helps to buffer the mood swings and stress that increase the risk of mind worm development.

At the time of writing, the COVID-19 pandemic is at its peak and most areas are under lockdown, preventing in-person social gatherings. Strive to stay in touch with friends online. I find tying this interaction to another activity will ensure some regularity to this socialization, whether that be video games, studying, or working on a group project.

Proliferation

Finally, let’s look at some activities that will stimulate the production and growth of mind worms, things we generally don’t want to do!

Social Media and Trivial Distractions

An instinctual response to the development of a mind worm is to distract oneself from the busyness of their mind by turning to social media. From my experience, trying to kill a mind worm with social media is like trying to wipe your butt with a paintbrush: its ineffective, highly irritating, and will just spread the problem around. The problem with this non-solution is that it is so accessible.

Personally, I don’t like spending my scarce willpower to suppress the compulsion to check my phone when I have a mind worm. I find the best way around this is to keep my phone in a completely different room of the house (up two flights of stairs). Humans don’t like expending energy, thus by increasing the amount of work required to check social media, it is far easier to break this bad habit.

I have only talked about social media here, but I feel like any trivial distraction with little auxiliary benefits falls into this same category 5. I suppose these could be particular to who you are as a person, but identify them and eliminate them! How do you know if something is a trivial distraction? Ask yourself this question: would you feel good at the end of the day having spent an hour doing that thing?
