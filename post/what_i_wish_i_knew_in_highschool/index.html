What I Wish I Knew In Highschool

Published February 15, 2021

There are some things that I wish my 9th-grade self knew. Here I have collected some of the “wisdom” I have acquired since then.

Ignore criticism about things you can’t change

This applies to your truly immutable characteristics and the traits that make you you. The former category consists of attributes like height, race, and looks, while the latter includes your passions, personality, and preferences.

I don’t mean not to listen to any constructive criticism; tough love can motivate beneficial change. However, filtering useless judgment will allow you to focus on what matters and deal with the social pressure (and absolute assholes) in highschool.

Follow passions, not people

It is too easy to get wrapped up in the pursuits, accomplishments, and popularity of others. Instead of transiently jumping between the interests of others, focus on the things you are passionate about. Expressing your passion is inspiring, and (most) people will respect and be drawn to you because of it.

In small doses inspiration from others is healthy, any more will end up being a distraction.

Think long term

Ignore daily noise to stay focused on longer-term goals. Obsession over present worries is both a cause and symptom of losing larger perspective.

Track personal progress over months and years, not daily variability. Your trend-line matters, not your outliers. I suggest reading Nassim Taleb’s Fooled By Randomness for more on this topic.

Another thing to watch for is the End-of-History Illusion, a cognitive bias where “individuals of all ages believe that they have experienced significant personal growth and changes in tastes up to the present moment, but will not substantially grow or mature in the future.” Its cliche, but maintaining a growth mindset is both powerful and difficult.

Recognize “signaling”

Signaling is where someone tries to convey information (the signal) to another person or group. Many things in life are motivated by the desire to send a signal about yourself to others 1. Recognize the things in your life that are done only for the purposes of signaling. Are you doing 35 extra-curricular activities, all at a surface level, just so it looks good on an application? One of the only decisions I regret 2 was made purely for signaling purposes.

Develop habits that will serve you for the rest of your life

Your mind is like a sponge that gets saturated with age. The habits you develop now, both good and bad, will stick with you for the rest of your life lest you invest the (usually immense) effort to wring them out. I won’t prescribe specific things you should or should not do, however I urge you to evaluate your routines and patterns.

Build your support system

Identify the people (family, friends, peers) that support you and your endeavours unconditionally. Often, these types of people will help you without the need for direct reciprocation. Remove (or reduce) relationships that feel transactional. Cultivate a mutually-supporting network of friends and family; it will last you for life.
