When to Stop?

2021-01-20

The prevailing worldview in western (capitalist) societies is that upward growth is both necessary and inevitable. By upward, I mean up the ladder of technological progress, the kind of progress Nick Bostrom refers to in his Vulnerable World Hypothesis

I would argue that, despite our propensity to always desire more, the technology and quality of life cultivated in wealthy cultures in the western world are already good enough, and upward progress should already be (mostly) in our rear-view mirror.

Instead, I think we should divert our attention to lateral growth, meaning the distribution of this quality of life and technology to the rest of the world, instead of working to further enhance the technology we already have. I feel the marginal reduction in suffering (from a utilitarian standpoint), would be far greater in pursing lateral rather than upward growth.

Substrate Neutrality

2021-01-25

In reviewing the suggested reading this week by Depew 3, I was really taken by the idea of “substrate neutrality” put forth by Daniel Dennet. In Dennet’s conception, natural selection is a formal algorithmic process that can be run on carbon or silicon-based life forms, making it substrate neutral. I think analyzing this concept in light of the discussion of the computational/machinic view of the human body could be quite enlightening. Questions arise like “what really separates biological life from machines?” and “at what point in the development of AI systems will we have to treat them as conscious like ourselves?”

While we tend to apply machinic models to biological life, I think the reverse will not be something that comes so easily, namely ascribing life, consciousness, and moral worth to non-biological systems. Just like the anthropocentrism that arises from Cartesian dualism, we will tend to be carbon-centric when it comes to what we consider to be living. This could present a dangerous dilemma if AI systems can really be conscious like humans.

An Interesting Convergence

2021-01-28

During my review of the reading by Casey 4, there was an interesting trend that I thought I would highlight: the convergence of artificial and natural systems. This is occurring on both fronts:

  1. Through the mutually reinforcing development of science and technology, our understanding of the natural has become mechanized. The models we use to describe biological systems are are those of mechanical systems (ie. the cybernetic description of the body 4 or the machine model of proteins5).
  1. The ever-deepening integration of technology into society, combined with the cultural normalization of humans as biological machines, has lead to an anthropization of technological systems. This is especially visible with the modern development of AI systems where we ascribe traits and abilities that were previously reserved for humans (like learning, prediction, creativity, and perhaps in the future consciousness).

With the further development of AI systems (bringing technology closer to nature) and our ever-deepening understanding of engineering the human body (bringing nature closer to technology), I can’t help but wonder if, when, and where (on the theoretical spectrum from nature to technology), this convergence will occur. At some point (very, very) far in the future, will there remain a distinction between natural and artificial systems?

Transparency in AI systems

2021-01-31

Transparency of AI is an open research challenge that is referred to as eXplainable AI (XAI). As noted, the most popular contemporary AI systems are Deep Learning models that rely on upwards of 100 billion parameters (a function input that the system is trying to optimize). These parameter spaces are not interpretable by the researchers that created them, let alone end users/customers of a potential future AI system. Thus, there is a push for AI systems to become more transparent: the system can represent and explain its “thought processes” in a human-understandable fashion.

The push towards XAI is becoming more important as AI invades ever more high-stakes areas of life, including healthcare and law. “In general, humans are reticent to adopt techniques that are not directly interpretable, tractable and trustworthy” [source]. Transparency should be just as much of a focus as performance if we are to ever see widespread AI implementation in society.

Philosophical Zombies

2021-01-31

This comes to the interesting thought experiment of Philosophical Zombies (p-zombie). Consider a being (like an advanced AI system) that behaves like a living conscious creature. Is it possible that such a being could be a p-zombie, merely imitating consciousness without actually being sentient?

This type of argument is often used to support Cartesian mind-body dualism. Proponents argue that p-zombies could exists, and that their existence would necessitate a distinction between the physical (res-extensa) and the mind (res-cogitans). Others, like Dennett, think that for such a p-zombie to have the remarkable ability to perfectly imitate a conscious being, it would have to be conscious itself.

These are interesting topics to consider when designing a test to distinguish between the conscious and unconscious, the natural and artificial, and an AI and a human (like the Turing Test).

Anthropomorphism of AI

2021-01-31

Materialized Refiguration as described by Myers5 is the idea that figurative tropes and metaphors can become literalized over time; instantiated in physical systems and practices. While Myers applies this concept to machinic analogies of biological systems, I think it can be used to examine the anthropomorphic attributes ascribed to AI.

What are the (positive or negative) consequences behind the anthropomorphization of AI systems? I would speculate that this affects AI research directions, public perceptions, and end-user ethical considerations.

Research may be driven towards mimicry of biological intelligence, a strategy that has seen mutual success in the fields of AI and neuroscience. By the same token, we have to be wary of thinking inside the biological box because of the structural differences between biological and artificial systems and to not limit our search space based on anthropocentrism.

I think that the anthropomorphism is misleading the public perception of AI. This manifests in an overblown fears, unchecked optimism, and the attribution of morality to (as yet) unconscious entities. It should be the responsibility of researchers and science communicators to temper the (I suspect growing) tendency to anthropomorphise AI to keep the perception of its capabilities and potential as objective as possible.
